Contributing to Open-Source

Hi, everyone! It's great to be here!

This talk is about contributing to open-source, why it's important to contribute, how would that help us and how to start contributing. Let's just get started with a bit of history.

Open-source in 1998

It was a different time back in the late 90's when the open-source term was coined and made popular. Before the term open-source there was the so-called free software. It's important to understand why the effort was made to make the open-source term and open-source software popular.

Halloween documents

This was the time of the Halloween documents — leaked internal reports from Microsoft showing how a big software company having dominance on the market was aiming to diminish open-source software and to impede its growth.

Open Source Initiative

The Open Source Initiative was started by Bruce Perens and Eric Raymond. They tried to help open-source software with its marketing problem. Free software was mistaken as just being without cost instead of providing freedom. Open-source and the initiatives around it helped create the image of open development, distribution and modification of the software in the minds of the public.

Open-source today

Open source is now ubiquitous. Everyone is using it. From big corporations to high-school students in developing nations. Even non-open-source software is using hundreds of open-source libraries. We know open-source is not going anywhere.

GitHub has released its state of the Octoverse a couple of months ago. They said the organisation with the largest number of open-source contributors is Microsoft. If you say that to people from the past they will laugh at you.

I would say the problems open-source was facing before are now mostly solved. Small teams, big corporations and governments all are fine with using open-source and embrace it even more with time. The adoption of open-source is not a problem. Every new developer that starts programming is using it — from the .NET stack to the latest JavaScript package manager.

We have a different problem now

But with more and more new people diving into programming, we have more and more users and relatively fewer and fewer contributors.

Many of the people from the generation which grew open-source to what it is today were contributing heavily. We need new people to do the same.

Also there are experienced people who have something to contribute, but for one reason or another they hold back.

Why to contribute?

Learn from the best

Learn from people better than you or even the best in a particular field.

Communication

Improve your communication. You'd learn to express yourself better, defend your opinions with arguments and accept others'.

Tools and best practices

Learn new tools and best practices. Many people are introduced to continuous integration, automated tests, workflows,code style checkers and fixers while exploring and contributing to open-source projects.

Problem-solving

Learn new approaches of solving problems. You could acquire a lot of useful skills by reading the discussions, looking at and suggesting alternative implementations, dealing with backwards and future compatibility.

Personal image

Improve your own personal image before your peers and employers. You will get more visibility and be able to present some of your good work to the public.

Remote work

You'll get better at remote work, creating software together with people in different time zones, communicating your intentions explicitly and keeping progress in the open.

Save the world

You can help making the world a better place. There are numerous open-source projects helping with charities, medical research, space exploration, human rights like privacy and voting.

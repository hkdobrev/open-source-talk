Contributing to Open-Source

Hi, everyone! It's great to be here!

This talk is about contributing to open-source, why it's important to contribute, how would that help us and how to start contributing. Let's just get started with a bit of history.

Open-source in 1998

It was a different time back in the late 90's when the open-source term was coined and made popular. Before the term open-source there was the so-called free software. It's important to understand why the effort was made to make the open-source term and open-source software popular.

Halloween documents

This was the time of the Halloween documents — leaked internal reports from Microsoft showing how a big software company having dominance on the market was aiming to diminish open-source software and to impede its growth.

Open Source Initiative

Then the Open Source Initiative was started by Bruce Perens and Eric Raymond. They tried to help open-source software with its marketing problem. Free software was mistaken as just being without cost instead of providing freedom. Open-source and the initiatives around it helped create the image of open development, distribution and modification of the software in the minds of the public.

Open-source today

Open source is now ubiquitous. Everyone is using it. From big corporations to high-school students in developing nations. Even non-open-source software is using hundreds of open-source libraries. We know open-source is not going anywhere.

GitHub has released its state of the Octoverse a couple of months ago. They said the organisation with the largest number of open-source contributors is Microsoft. If you say that to people from the past, they will laugh at you.

I would say the problems open-source was facing before are now mostly solved. Small teams, big corporations and governments all embracing it and this just increases even more with time. The adoption of open-source is not a problem.

We have a different problem now

But with more and more new people diving into programming, we have more and more users and relatively fewer and fewer contributors.

Many of the people from the generation which grew open-source to what it is today were contributing heavily. We need new people to do the same.

Also, there are experienced people who have something to contribute, but for one reason or another they hold back.

Open-source is taken for granted

Open-source is taken for granted. Every new developer who starts programming is using it — from the .NET stack to the latest JavaScript package manager.

But the question is whether the majority of them would join the open-source community

Maintainers ≤ Contributors < Users

This is a formula which I believe holds true. The number of contributors of a repository is greater than or equal to the number of maintainers. The number of users however is significantly greater than the number of contributors.

And this will hold true even if more people join open-source. But we need to make sure the gap between number of contributors and number of users does not increase materially.

We need more contributors!

So we need to activate more developers from the existing IT community and the new people coming in. Even people who are not programmers or not technical at all could help.

But why to contribute?

But why should people contribute? What would motivate them?

Contribute, because we need more contributors? No, that doesn't work! That's not a motivation.

Giving back to the community?

Giving back to the community? Some people are indeed motivated by that, but I think this comes later when you explore the beauty of the open-source community more. And a lot would not consider this a driving motive.

For the greater good?

Many people think contributing to open-source means doing work without payment for the greater good. And this is true in many cases, but that's not the only thing. You are getting a lot in return as well.

contribute because it helps
YOU

I want to make the case for the idea that open-source contributions could help you. It's often seen as a selfless act, but it also brings you a lot of benefits and self-improvements.

Contributing to open-source is not a zero-sum game. When you use open-source it doesn't loose anything and when you contribute back you gain as well.

Benefits from contributing

So I want to share some practical benefits for you which I think you would get if you actively contribute to open-source.

Learn from the best

You can learn from people better than you or even the best in a particular field.

Communication

You will improve your communication. You'd learn to express yourself better, defend your opinions with arguments and accept others'.

I can't stress this enough! Most people who don't have long experience in open-source or remote work cannot express themselves well in writing and especially when communicating with people they don't know.

In my opinion this is a key skill and it needs to be sharpened whenever possible.

Tools and best practices

You will learn about new tools and best practices. Many people are introduced to things like: automated tests, continuous integration, code style checkers and fixers while exploring and contributing to open-source projects.

Workflows and processes

You will find out about: releases and release branches, changelogs, public discussions, requests for comments, code reviews.

Problem-solving

Learn new approaches to solving problems. You could acquire a lot of useful skills by reading the discussions, looking at and suggesting alternative implementations, dealing with backwards and future compatibility.

Personal image

You will improve your own personal image before your peers and employers. You will get more visibility and be able to present some of your good work to the public.

Many employers look for open-source contributions when considering whether to hire you. This may not be always fair, but it is common. However, the skills you will acquire and the things you will learn during your open-source contributions would actually make you a better candidate as well, not only the number of pull requests, stars or followers.

Remote work

You'll get significantly better at remote work. You will see how software is being created asynchronously together with hundreds of individuals in different time zones. You will start communicating your intentions explicitly. You will understand why keeping progress in the open is important and how to do it yourself.

Save the world

You can help making the world a better place. There are numerous open-source projects helping with charities, medical research, space exploration and human rights like privacy and voting.

How to contribute?

Start now

Start now. As I said before, scratch your own itch. So start with something you use. Whether it is your favourite PHP framework, Ruby gem, or JavaScript package. Chances are, you already use an open-source library which you are quite familiar with than most people. There could be a small feature you are missing or a nasty bug that's bothering you. The important is to start now. Communicate with the maintainers the bug, the feature, the documentation fix and get involved now!

Impostor syndrome

The impostor syndrome is quite common. It describes the state of mind when you have an unrealistic low self-esteem and feel fear of being exposed. Many people feel it at work and it is also common in open-source. People stop themselves from contributing, because they think they are not worthy and don't have anything meaningful to contribute.

In the IT industry this is especially common as there are always people smarter than you and they are quite visible. However, you need to believe in yourself.

There is always something you can contribute. From translations, to issues, to ground work.

Your company doesn't allow open-source

Some people say their company wouldn't allow them to open-source anything even personal projects in their free time. But is this really true? Ask your boss, manager, human resources or a company lawyer. Chances are you are free to contribute to open-source projects. Even if you don't you can ask for a permission. Don't find excuses, just act. Also, don't forget to think about whether your employer shares your interests and is best for you.
